This is a very cool idea, bringing in all the messy voodoo stuff into the English kitchen. I would suggest you try setting it in present tense, however – the dialogue is so immediate that it is not clear if the past tense is used because Mother Nastri is dead, or because the story is told in past tense.

I enjoyed the rawness and juxtaposition of images, but was a bit bothered by some of the punctuation, spelling and phrasing.

> “…Please come in it’s nice to meet you in person. “
Definitely a comma, or even a full stop, after “come in”. There needs to be a pause.

>“… for the dogs”
It looks a bit naked without the customary comma, or even a full stop. “… for the dogs.”

> – boiled down chicken blood doesn’t smell like tripe but Mrs Peirson wouldn’t know that.
Again, comma before but.

>“You say cloves? And cinnamon? Well it is a kitchen…”
I would recommend comma after well, or emphasis on IS for a different sentence flow. “Well, it is a kitchen…” or “Well it IS a kitchen…” (sounds a bit more colloquial but still works ok.)

> – scorched bat fur smells nothing like cloves!
That sentence is a bit awkward – the repetition of cloves is perhaps unnecessary. “That would be the scorched bat fur, I suspect.” or something like that works better.

>Mother Nastri would have cackled wildly at that: She was quietly [don't need the "quietly", it does not fit in with how she speaks] proud of the strange reeks that issued from her quarters behind the main house. [this is where i would suggest present tense, for example.] In those long forgotten years in Haiti such stenches advertised her power and artifice. [this reference is a bit confusing – it needs contextualising, like “but now it’s all tripe and cloves to the English.”

> – it had better be: I spend
spent

>blood slick rope
what does that mean? Is it a type of rope or do you mean “rope slick with blood”?

>I was ["had been", or keep the was but change the rest to present tense] taught ,

>“…Earl Grey for you dear …”
Comma before “dear”.

>“My mind-technique is simple – have a look at this rope, run it though your hands, concentrate on it”
Could try pacing out the instructions with full stops to feel less rushed.

>– a real hangman’s noose, used a decade ago to execute a certain John Muller, not easy to acquire in civilised England.
I’d suggest splitting out the last phrase on its own. “…John Muller. Not easy to acquire…”

>“Oh! I am so sorry Mrs Peirson
Comma before Mrs.

>“But you have the hang of it Mrs Peirson – just remember the mind-technique every night and your pregnancy is assured”
Comma before Mrs, full stop after assured.

What, me? Picky?
